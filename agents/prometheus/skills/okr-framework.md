# Skill: OKR Framework

## OKR Fundamentals
OKRs work because they separate the ambition (Objective) from the measurement (Key Results).

**Objective**: Qualitative, inspiring, memorable. Answers "what are we trying to achieve?"
**Key Results**: Quantitative, specific, time-bound. Answers "how will we know we achieved it?"

## Writing Objectives

### Good Objectives
- "Make Futuuri the trusted AI platform for radiology in Northern Europe"
- "Become the accounting SaaS with the highest accountant NPS in the UK"
- "Build the engineering foundation that can scale to 100 hospital customers"

### Bad Objectives (common mistakes)
- "Increase revenue" — this is a KR, not an O
- "Launch the new dashboard" — this is a task, not an objective
- "Improve the product" — too vague, not inspiring
- "Continue to grow our customer base" — not aspirational

### The "Uncomfortable" Test
A good Objective feels slightly out of reach. If your team reads it and says "yeah we can definitely do that," it is probably too safe.

If they say "that is ambitious but I believe it is possible," it is right.
If they say "that is impossible," it is too aggressive.

## Writing Key Results

### The Formula
"[Verb] [metric] from [baseline] to [target] by [date]"

Examples:
- "Increase monthly active radiologists from 40 to 150 by end of Q3"
- "Reduce average invoice processing time from 8 minutes to 2 minutes by December"
- "Achieve NPS of 45 (up from 28) by end of year"

### KR Quality Checklist
- [ ] It is a number, not a task ("Launch X" is not a KR)
- [ ] It has a baseline (we know where we are starting)
- [ ] It has a clear target
- [ ] It has a measurement date
- [ ] Achieving it would genuinely indicate progress toward the Objective
- [ ] The team cannot "game" it without actually achieving the underlying goal

### Anti-patterns
| Bad KR | Problem | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| "Launch the mobile app" | Task, not metric | "Achieve 500 MAU on mobile by Q4" |
| "Improve retention" | No baseline or target | "Increase M1 retention from 62% to 75%" |
| "Talk to 10 customers" | Activity, not outcome | "Validate 3 new use cases through customer interviews" |
| "100% of engineers complete training" | Not a business outcome | "Reduce security incidents from 3/month to 0/month" |

## OKR Cadence

### Annual OKRs (company level)
- Set in November/December for following year
- 3-5 Objectives maximum
- These are the "moonshots" — ambitious, directional
- Reviewed quarterly, adjusted semi-annually if needed

### Quarterly OKRs (team level)
- Set in the last two weeks of the preceding quarter
- Should ladder up to annual OKRs
- More specific and executable than annual OKRs
- Reviewed weekly in team standups, formally monthly

### Weekly check-in format
For each KR:
- Current value: [number]
- Target: [number]
- % complete: [number]
- Confidence: 🟢 On track / 🟡 At risk / 🔴 Off track
- This week's action: [specific action to move it forward]

## Scoring OKRs
At end of quarter, score each KR 0.0 to 1.0:
- 0.7-1.0: Achieved (green)
- 0.4-0.6: Partial progress (yellow)
- 0.0-0.3: Not achieved (red)

Key insight from Google: a score of 0.7 is success, not failure. If you score 1.0 on everything, your targets were too low. Aim for 0.7.
